# Writing Sample (MBA Dissertation)
(Revised excerpt submitted as a writing sample to demonstrate qualitative analysis and research discipline)

This sample is included only to evidence interpretive qualitative discipline and careful claims scoping; it is not a statement of the current HCI/CSCW research topic.

## Title

Assessing the Impact of FinTech Disruptive Capabilities on High Street Banks in the United Kingdom

## 1. Context and Research Question

This study examines how FinTech-driven capabilities reshape the socio-technical conditions of UK retail banking, with particular attention to service delivery practices, customer expectations, and the organisational constraints that shape user-facing systems. The purpose of this writing sample is to demonstrate analytical clarity, methodological discipline, and evidence-based reasoning using secondary qualitative data.

The UK retail banking sector provides an appropriate empirical context due to its mature regulatory environment, dense legacy infrastructure, and early exposure to digital challengers.

Research Question: How do FinTech disruptive capabilities reshape service delivery practices, cost structures, and organisational responses within incumbent UK high street banks, and what sector narratives describe as driving these shifts?

## 2. Method

The study adopts an interpretive qualitative research design based on secondary data analysis using publicly available sources. Data sources include industry reports, regulatory publications, policy documents, and peer-reviewed literature relevant to UK retail banking.

A purposive sampling strategy prioritised sources addressing FinTech-enabled capabilities such as digital payments, mobile banking platforms, open banking infrastructure, and automation. Sources were selected for analytical relevance rather than representativeness, reflecting an interpretive aim of identifying described mechanisms and patterns rather than estimating causal effects.

Analysis followed a thematic synthesis approach. Texts were coded iteratively, with initial descriptive codes refined into higher-order analytic themes through comparison across sources.

Methodological limitations were acknowledged at the outset. Reliance on secondary data restricts access to internal decision-making processes and limits causal inference. Claims should therefore be read as interpretive insights about how the sector is described, rather than predictive statements.

## 3. Findings and Analysis

### 3.1 Service Interaction Expectations and Operational Reconfiguration

Across the reviewed sources, FinTech capabilities are consistently associated with shifts in how banking services are delivered and experienced, including greater emphasis on self-service, immediacy, and transparency. These shifts are described as interacting with operational reconfiguration pressures, including reductions in marginal transaction costs and wider use of automation in routine processes.

Digital-first challengers are frequently described as operating with leaner infrastructure, which highlights inefficiencies embedded in branch-based operating models. Incumbent responses appear uneven, ranging from incremental upgrades layered onto existing systems to broader modernisation programmes, often discussed in relation to organisational readiness and constraints.

### 3.2 Reconfiguration of Service Practices and User Experience Priorities

FinTech platforms foreground immediacy, transparency, and self-service, reshaping customer expectations of banking service. User experience design is repeatedly positioned as a competitive differentiator rather than a peripheral concern.

Incumbent banks face constraints in replicating these experiences due to legacy systems and regulatory complexity. As a result, experience innovation is often described as lagging behind technological capability, with user-facing change shaped by back-end dependencies and compliance demands.

### 3.3 Heterogeneity in Organisational Responses Under Constraint

Strategic responses among incumbent banks vary widely, ranging from partnerships and acquisitions to internally developed innovation units. Across sources, these strategies are discussed as reflecting governance structures, organisational culture, and historical investment decisions that condition what change is feasible.

Rather than convergence, the sector is presented as exhibiting increasing heterogeneity in how incumbents interpret and respond to FinTech disruption, including differences in the pace and scope of service transformation.

## 4. Conclusion and Limits

This study demonstrates the ability to frame a researchable question, justify methodological choices, and derive analytic claims from qualitative evidence. FinTech disruption is described as operating through multiple interacting mechanisms affecting operations, customer experience expectations, and organisational change pathways.

Claims are limited to the UK retail banking context and to interpretive insights drawn from secondary data. The primary value of this work lies in methodological training in structured qualitative synthesis, careful boundary-setting of claims, and disciplined argumentation based on documentary evidence.

As a PhD writing sample, this excerpt is provided to evidence qualitative research discipline, transparent scoping of claims, and careful handling of interpretive limits.
